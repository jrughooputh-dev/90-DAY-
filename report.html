Nicotine from tobacco chronically elevates serum cortisol by 25–50%, directly driving visceral fat accumulation around the abdomen. Carbon monoxide from combustion reduces haemoglobin oxygen-carrying capacity, impairing both aerobic performance and cellular nutrient delivery. Tobacco suppresses muscle protein synthesis (MPS) by inhibiting mTORC1 pathway signalling — the primary cellular mechanism through which muscle is built. Daily THC via combustion disrupts slow-wave and REM sleep architecture, blunting the growth hormone pulse that occurs in the first 90 minutes of deep sleep. Combined, these two substances operating at 3–5 daily sessions ensure your recovery, body composition progress, and psoriasis management are all running at significantly below optimal capacity. This is the highest-priority intervention in this entire report.

Circadian misalignment is not simply a lifestyle inconvenience — it has measurable physiological consequences. Growth hormone, which is essential for muscle repair and fat metabolism, is primarily secreted in the first sleep cycle before midnight. Sleeping at 5am guarantees this window is missed entirely. Cortisol follows a diurnal rhythm that peaks naturally between 6–8am regardless of when you sleep — waking at 10–11am means your cortisol spike occurs while you are still asleep or just waking, creating a physiological state of elevated stress without the corresponding alertness and food intake needed to manage it. This elevates catabolic activity (muscle breakdown) during your most vulnerable period. Sleep timing misalignment is also a confirmed independent trigger for psoriasis flares and reduces insulin sensitivity by up to 30% in chronic cases.

At 25 this is not yet a clinical metabolic risk, but it is the mechanism behind your current physique dissatisfaction and needs to be addressed before it calcifies as a pattern. Muscle Protein Synthesis & Recovery

Muscle protein synthesis (MPS) is the cellular process by which your body repairs and builds muscle fibres following training stimulus. It is driven by three inputs: mechanical stress (training), amino acid availability (diet), and anabolic hormonal signalling (sleep + hormones). You currently have suppression operating on all three channels via smoking, poor sleep, and low protein intake. The protocol below is designed to maximise MPS output within your current constraints while progressively removing the suppressors.

Recovery timeline — what happens after each session

0–30 minutes post-training
Anabolic window — protein priority
MPS is maximally elevated. Consume 30–40g fast-digesting protein within 45 minutes — whey protein shake, Greek yogurt, or eggs. Include 30–50g carbohydrates to replenish glycogen and spike insulin (which is anabolic post-training). This is your most impactful nutritional decision of the day.
30 min – 4 hours post-training
Acute inflammation phase — necessary but manageable
Microscopic muscle fibre tears trigger localised inflammation — this is normal and necessary for adaptation. Do not take NSAIDs (ibuprofen) in this window as they blunt the inflammatory signal required for muscle repair. Hydrate, rest, eat. Smoking in this window is particularly damaging as it impairs the vascular response needed for nutrient delivery to repairing tissue.
4–24 hours — sleep window
Growth hormone pulse — the real building phase
The majority of muscle repair occurs during sleep, specifically during slow-wave (deep) sleep when growth hormone is secreted in its largest daily pulse. Consuming casein protein or cottage cheese before sleep provides a sustained amino acid supply through this repair window — 6–8 hour slow digestion. Magnesium glycinate (300mg) taken 45 minutes before sleep deepens slow-wave sleep measurably. THC disrupts this phase — this is why recovery is blunted even when sleep duration is adequate.
24–72 hours — remodelling phase
Muscle supercompensation — where growth actually happens
Protein synthesis remains elevated for 24–72 hours after a training session. This is why consistent protein intake across all days — not just training days — matters. During this window the muscle is being remodelled slightly thicker and stronger than before the session. Soreness indicates active repair. Continue hitting protein targets. Do not train the same muscle group while significant soreness persists — you will be training already-depleted tissue.
48–72 hours — full recovery
Supercompensation complete — ready for next stimulus
The muscle group is now marginally stronger than before the session. This is the window to train it again. Training before this point (overtraining) produces diminishing returns. Training after this point (undertraining) means the supercompensation plateau is lost. Your 3-day-per-week schedule is timed correctly for this cycle at your current recovery capacity.

Lung & Respiratory Nutrition

Your lungs are under active oxidative stress from daily combustion. Nutrition cannot undo the structural damage of smoking — but it can significantly reduce the rate of damage, accelerate cellular repair, and improve functional respiratory capacity.

Combustion generates free radicals and reactive oxygen species (ROS) that damage lung epithelial cells and inflame bronchial tissue. The body's defence is the antioxidant system — Vitamin C, E, glutathione, and beta-carotene. Smoking depletes all of these simultaneously. The strategy is to aggressively replenish them through diet and targeted supplementation while reducing the oxidative load by reducing smoking frequency.

Psoriasis Management Protocol

Psoriasis is an immune-mediated inflammatory condition driven by dysregulated T-cell activity and overproduction of pro-inflammatory cytokines including TNF-α, IL-17, and IL-23. It is not curable through lifestyle alone — but its severity and flare frequency are directly modulated by the inflammatory load of the body as a whole. Every intervention in this report that reduces systemic inflammation — omega-3s, anti-inflammatory diet, improved sleep, reduced smoking — reduces psoriasis severity as a consequence.

Smoking — primary modifiable psoriasis trigger
Tobacco smoke directly activates the same immune pathways that drive psoriasis. Nicotine stimulates nicotinic acetylcholine receptors on keratinocytes (skin cells), triggering abnormal proliferation — the defining feature of psoriatic plaques. Smoking is associated with more severe psoriasis, resistance to treatment, and higher rates of psoriatic arthritis development. This is biochemically the most direct intervention available for reducing your psoriasis severity.
Tbilisi sun — use it therapeutically
Narrow-band UVB phototherapy is a first-line medical treatment for psoriasis — Tbilisi's sun provides a natural version of this. 15–20 minutes of direct midday sun on affected skin, building from 10 minutes initially, is clinically equivalent to mild phototherapy. Do this consistently 4–5x per week from May through September. Do not burn — sunburn triggers a Koebner phenomenon (psoriasis appearing at sites of skin trauma). Apply sunscreen to unaffected areas if exposure exceeds 20 minutes.
Anti-inflammatory diet specifics for psoriasis
Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA from salmon and mackerel) directly reduce the IL-17 and TNF-α signalling that drives psoriatic inflammation. Curcumin from turmeric inhibits NF-κB — a transcription factor that is overactive in psoriasis. Quercetin from apples and onions reduces mast cell degranulation. These are not supplementary additions — they are functional therapeutic agents for your condition.
Post-training skin protocol
Sweat is mildly acidic and contains urea, lactic acid, and salts that irritate active psoriatic plaques, particularly in flexural areas. Shower within 20 minutes of finishing every training session using a fragrance-free, sulphate-free body wash at lukewarm temperature. Apply a thick fragrance-free emollient (Cetaphil, Eucerin, or equivalent) within 3 minutes of stepping out — this is the optimal window when the stratum corneum is still hydrated. For active plaques, apply prescribed topical treatment before the emollient layer.
Stress management — cortisol is a direct trigger
Cortisol downregulates regulatory T-cells that normally suppress the aberrant immune response in psoriasis. Chronic cortisol elevation — from poor sleep, smoking, life stress — removes the brake on the immune dysregulation. Every positive sleep, exercise, and dietary change in this protocol reduces cortisol chronically and consequently reduces psoriasis flare frequency. The connection is direct and bidirectional: better lifestyle → lower cortisol → fewer flares → less inflammatory load → better recovery → better training results.
